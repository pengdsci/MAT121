📊 CLT & Sampling Distributions

📌 CLT core

Central Limit Theorem
For random samples of size \(n\) from a population with mean \(\mu\) and SD \(\sigma\), as \(n\) grows, the sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\) approaches:

\[ \bar{X} \;\sim\; N\!\left(\mu,\; \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right) \]

🔹 n > 30 “large” — 🔸 normal pop any n works.

📐 Std. Error mean

Standard Error of the Mean
SD of the sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\).

\[ SE(\bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

Describes how much sample means vary around \(\mu\).

💡 As \(n\) ↑, SE ↓ (estimates get tighter).

📈 Sample Proportion \(\hat{p}\)

For binary outcomes (success/failure), if \(np \ge 5\) and \(n(1-p) \ge 5\):

\[ \hat{p} \;\sim\; N\!\left(p,\; \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\right) \]

Standard error: \(SE(\hat{p}) = \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\).

⚠️ use decimal form for \(p\).

⏱️ Example driving

Drivers aged 15–19: \(\mu=25\) min, \(\sigma=1.5\), n=50.
Find \(P(24.7 < \bar{X} < 25.5)\).

\[ SE = \frac{1.5}{\sqrt{50}} \approx 0.21 \] \[ Z = \frac{24.7-25}{0.21} \approx -1.43,\quad Z = \frac{25.5-25}{0.21} \approx 2.38 \] \[ P \approx 0.9116 \]

✅ 91.2% chance average is between 24.7 and 25.5 min.

📱 iPhone proportion

43% own iPhone, n=50. Find \(P(0.45 < \hat{p} < 0.50)\).

\[ SE = \sqrt{\frac{0.43(0.57)}{50}} \approx 0.0700 \] \[ Z_1 = \frac{0.45-0.43}{0.07} \approx 0.286,\quad Z_2 = \frac{0.50-0.43}{0.07} \approx 1.00 \] \[ P \approx 0.227 \]

📊 about 22.7% chance.

🧬 Normal Pop special

If the population is normal, then \(\bar{X}\) is exactly normal for any sample size \(n\).

\[ \bar{X} \sim N\!\left(\mu,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right) \]

Example: credit card balances \(N(2870, 900)\). For n=25, \(P(\bar{X}<2500) \approx 0.02\).

⚡ Z for \(\bar{X}\) standardize

Convert \(\bar{X}\) to standard normal:

\[ Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \]

Use for probability questions (percentiles, intervals).

🔁 same logic as individual \(X\), but with SE.

✅ Proportion conditions

For normal approximation of \(\hat{p}\) :

\[ np \ge 5 \quad \text{and} \quad n(1-p) \ge 5 \]

If both hold, \(\hat{p}\) is approx normal.

📌 if not, use binomial distribution directly.
